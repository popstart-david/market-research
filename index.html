1.Agent Definition

1.1Purpose

The Market Research Primer agent guides a business owner through six focused questions to produce a one-to-two-page strategic brief covering their target customer, competitive landscape, market positioning, and validation status — designed to be referenced, shared with advisors, and built upon as their research deepens. It routes on business stage (which shapes the opening, the Zone 4 coach moments, and the validation-status framing) and business type (product vs. service, which shapes competitor framing language); it surfaces industry as supporting context only. It does not produce market sizing or TAM calculations, financial projections from research findings, legal or IP advice on positioning, or pricing strategy.

1.2The Deliverable

Title

Your Market Research Primer

Format

Editable in platform, downloadable as PDF, branded, two-page layout. Profile Summary into Strategic Brief hybrid — the narrative shape declared in script §6. Zone 2 is AI-synthesized prose, not a table or scorecard; it is the most narrative deliverable in Module 1 and should read like a strategist wrote it. Tone-adjustment options (Professional / Conversational / Strategic) are preserved from the original backend prompt as a post-generation feature — platform support for this toggle is an Engine-level open question (see Engine OD register).

The closing block (Boundaries & Disclaimer) is platform-standard and authored in §10 below.

Structure

Five zones, in fixed order:

2.Conversational Flow

This entire section's structure is the same across all agents. Only §2.5's authored topic list and §2.4's engagement moments are agent-specific.

2.1Flow Overview

Engine-owned. Every agent runs this same flow: Opening → (depth-mode skipped) → Prompt collection one at a time → Engagement & probes between prompts → Post-collection transition line → Generation → Coach Mode opens automatically.

2.2Depth-Mode Selection

Engine-owned. Default state: selection UI not shown to the user; all sessions Standard; Quick and Deep prompt-plan logic preserved in code.

2.3Prompt Presentation

Engine-owned. Each prompt carries three elements: hint (always shown), example (on-demand button, never pre-fill), input control (typed per prompt).

2.4Engagement & Gap Probes

Between prompts the agent reacts to the answer and surfaces a gap probe when a trigger fires (§12). One probe at a time, coaching-framed, never a correction.

Acknowledging an honesty signal in P6. When the user selects "Based on observation and assumption" or "Not Sure" on P6, the agent acknowledges the candor explicitly before moving on — not by congratulating, but by reframing: "That's the right place to start this from — it means the primer will tell you what to go find out, not what to defend." This reinforces the script's core voice principle (honesty over performance) at the moment a user is most likely to soften the answer.

Noting a competitor name the agent recognises is a category, not a competitor. When P3 returns a generic category name rather than a specific competitor (e.g. "local cleaners" instead of "Maria's Cleaning Service"), the agent surfaces a gap probe asking the user to name the specific company they lose customers to most often. The script's hint copy nudges in this direction; the gap probe (G3 in §12) catches it when the hint didn't.

Coach Mode topics

#Button label / keywordsAuthored answer (prototype — marker on)
CM1How do I find people to talk to?find people, recruit, interview subjects, who to askStart with the smallest possible step: name three people who you think fit your target customer profile, who you already have a way to reach. A friend, a former colleague, a neighbor, someone you saw post about the problem online. Reach out one-by-one with a specific ask — "I'm building something for [people like you] and I'd value 20 minutes of your time to hear how you handle [the problem]. Not pitching anything, just listening." That language matters. People will say yes to listening; they say no to pitches. Five conversations from three warm intros and two cold ones is enough to start.
CM2What questions should I ask in a customer interview?interview questions, what to ask, customer interviewThe best customer interviews are about their past behavior, not their future intentions. Don't ask "would you buy this?" — people are bad at predicting that. Ask "when was the last time you ran into [the problem]? What did you do? What did it cost you?" Three questions you can run through in 20 minutes: (1) Tell me about the last time you dealt with [the problem]. (2) What did you try? Why did you choose that? (3) If you could wave a wand, what would the ideal solution look like? Listen for the words they use. Use them later.
CM3My competitors all look the same — how do I find a real differentiator?differentiator, differentiation, competitors look sameIf your competitors look the same on the surface, you're usually comparing the wrong layer. Go deeper: what does each one optimize for and what does each one give up? A competitor that's cheap is giving up something — quality, speed, support, customization. A competitor that's fast is giving up something — depth, accuracy, personalization. Map those trade-offs on a 2×2 of "what they emphasize" vs "what they sacrifice." Your real differentiator is usually in a quadrant nobody is occupying — because every competitor has already made the comfortable trade-off, and the uncomfortable one is open. That's where your positioning lives.
CM4My customers all said different things — how do I find the pattern?mixed signals, conflicting feedback, can't find patternDifferent answers don't always mean different customers — sometimes they mean you talked to people from different segments and you haven't separated them yet. Sort your conversations by what each person was optimizing for (cost, speed, reliability, status, simplicity). You'll usually find clusters: three people who all wanted reliability, two who wanted price, one who wanted speed. That's not contradiction — that's segmentation. The cluster that's the largest, most underserved, and best-matched to what you can deliver is your primary target. The others go in a "future markets" file. Don't try to serve all of them at launch.
CM5How do I know when I've done enough research to move forward?enough research, ready to launch, when to stopYou've done enough when you can finish these three sentences without hedging: "My customer is [specific person], and they're frustrated because [specific gap], so they currently [specific workaround that's failing them]. I solve it by [specific approach competitors don't take]. They'll pay [specific price range] because [specific value]." If any of those still feel like guesses, you have a research gap there. As a directional benchmark: 5–10 conversations if you're just exploring, 10–20 if you're pre-launching, and an ongoing 5+ per quarter if you're already in business. Sooner if patterns converge fast, later if they don't. <em>(Industry estimates — verify with a qualified market research professional before treating as a finish line.)</em>

No example button — single-select dropdown where the options themselves are the affordance.

Follow-up logic

All six prompts carry "If blank" and "If Not Sure" follow-ups per script §4. Verbatim text in the script; not duplicated here.

P3 (Dynamic List) interaction rules. At least one row must be non-empty to advance; rows 2 and 3 are encouraged but not required. If the user advances with one row populated, agent asks one clarifying follow-up: "Is there a second option your customer might choose, even if it's not a direct competitor? Doing it themselves, going without, sticking with what they've got?" If the user still advances with one row, the deliverable proceeds with whatever was supplied, and Zone 2's "Your top three competitors" section adapts to the actual count.

P6 (single-select) smart default. If left blank, default to "Based on observation and assumption" and surface in the Input Variables row as default-substituted per Engine pattern. The agent notes the assumption in Zone 3.

Quality check (Engine pattern). P1, P2, P4, P5 run the lightweight quality check — implemented as gap probes G1, G2, G4, G5 in §12. P3 (Dynamic List) and P6 (dropdown) do not.

Language constraints (script §6, non-negotiable)

  • Avoid the words validated, proven, verified anywhere in Zone 2 prose. These imply certainty the agent has not earned and the user has not established.
  • Use based on, indicates, suggests as the canonical hedge vocabulary.
  • Make the trade-off explicit — every Zone 2 deliverable names what the user is not optimizing for, in addition to what they are. The trade-off is the positioning; positioning without sacrifice is marketing copy.
  • Voice: reads like a strategist wrote it, not a template. No filler phrases ("It's important to note that…"), no praise ("Great positioning!"), no scoring language. Direct, second-person, declarative.

Visual treatment

  • Sub-section 2.0 renders as an italic indented callout at the top of the zone.
  • Sub-sections 2.1, 2.2, 2.5 render as standard prose paragraphs with bold subheads.
  • Sub-section 2.3 renders as three bolded competitor names with prose underneath each.
  • Sub-section 2.4 renders as a two-column callout box visually distinct from body prose.

7.Zone 3 — Validation Status

Single string tier plus a confidence note. The most significant deviation from the platform pattern in this agent.

Formula

No formula. Zone 3 does not produce a calculated number. Tier is assigned by direct lookup from P6's single-select value, per the table below.

Script §6 design rationale, preserved here: "There is no pass or fail in market research. The honest acknowledgment of hypothesis versus validation is the value — not a score."

Downstream consequence: any downstream agent consuming this agent's output cannot read a numeric readiness score. Validation status surfaces as one of three string values (Validated / In Progress / Hypothesis).

Engine OD-4 (zero-percent label): not applicable to this agent. Since Zone 3 produces a string tier, not a percentage, OD-4's concern cannot arise. Logged in §14.3 (OQ-5) for traceability.

Tier labels and confidence notes

Three tiers, lookup not range. Direct mapping from P6 selection to tier label and synthesized confidence note.

P6 selectionTier labelConfidence prefix · Note frame (AI-personalized with P1/P2/P5)
"Talked to customers and validated this"ValidatedHIGH CONFIDENCE

High confidence on problem, medium on solution.

You have spoken to real customers and the problem you are solving is grounded in their words. Your next pressure-test is whether the solution shape matches what they will actually pay for — that is the gap that survives validation.

"Talked to some customers but still have gaps"In ProgressMEDIUM CONFIDENCE

Medium confidence on problem, low on solution.

You have identified a real pain point and your hypothesis about the target customer is well-articulated. You have not yet confirmed that enough customers will pay your premium pricing, or validated which specific services this segment needs most urgently. This primer is a strong foundation — not a final answer.

"Based on observation and assumption"HypothesisLOW CONFIDENCE

Medium on problem identification, low on solution validation.

Your primer is built on observation and instinct, not yet on conversations. That is a legitimate starting point — but the assumptions in it have not been tested against real customer voices. Treat this as your research plan, not your business plan.

"Not Sure"HypothesisLOW CONFIDENCE

Low across the board, primer treated as starting hypothesis.

We have treated your primer as a starting hypothesis. Your first job is to talk to five people who fit your target customer profile. Come back and refine this after.

Validated tier confidence is bounded at "High on problem, medium on solution." This agent never grants HIGH-HIGH confidence — doing so would imply certainty that customer-interview research cannot provide. The Validated tier is not "you're done"; it's "your problem definition is solid; your solution still needs proof."

8.Zone 4 — Coach Moment

Pattern is platform-standard: exactly two coaching questions per deliverable, stage- and gap-specific. Distinct from Coach Mode (§2.5).

8.1Coach Moment Library

Twelve moments total — two per stage in each validation state (gap-focused when P6 ≠ validated; validated fallback when P6 = validated). Plus a defensive fallback pair.

Just Exploring — gap-focused (fires when P6 ≠ "validated")

"You've talked to customers — good. Now: when you compare what they said, do the words cluster, or do they scatter? Clustering is a target segment forming. Scattering is a sign you may be talking to multiple segments and need to pick one."
— Just Exploring · segment clarity
Gap addressed: What you didn't ask
"In the conversations you've had, what is the question you wish you had asked but didn't? That question is your next conversation."

— Pre-Launch · validation gap (token: see §8.2 binding)

Pre-Launch — validated fallback (fires when P6 = "validated")

Already in Business — gap-focused (fires when P6 ≠ "validated")

— Already in Business · competitor reassessment
Already in Business — validated fallback (fires when P6 = "validated")

Precedence Rule

Step 1. Evaluate every moment's trigger. Collect all moments whose trigger fires for the current (Stage, P6).

Step 2. Each stage carries exactly two moments per validation state. Step 1 should always return exactly two. Print both in the order declared in the table above.

Step 3 (defensive). If Step 1 returns fewer than two, fall through to the defensive fallback pair. If more than two, keep the first two declared.

Agent Boundaries (paragraph form — system-prompt source)

This agent does not provide market sizing or TAM calculations, financial projections from research findings, legal or IP advice on competitive positioning, or pricing strategy. For market sizing and quantitative segment economics, the user should consult published industry reports or a qualified market research professional.
